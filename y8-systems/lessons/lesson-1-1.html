Lesson 1-1: What Makes a Society?

Understanding Systems Foundation Through Indigenous & Contemporary Lenses

Students explore what constitutes a society by examining different community structures, from traditional Māori iwi systems to modern nation-states, understanding that societies are complex systems built on relationships, values, and shared decision-making.

Whakapapa - The Foundation of Society

In Te Ao Māori, society isn't just a collection of individuals - it's a web of relationships stretching back through generations and forward to descendants not yet born. Whakapapa (genealogical connections) provides the foundation for understanding how communities form, maintain themselves, and make decisions together. Every society, whether traditional or modern, is built on similar principles of connection, shared responsibility, and collective wellbeing.

Ngā Whāinga Ako - Learning Intentions

Students Will Understand

  • Societies as systems with interconnected components
  • Different ways communities organize and govern themselves
  • How values shape societal structures and decisions
  • The role of collective responsibility in community wellbeing

Students Will Apply

  • Compare different societal structures and governance models
  • Analyze how communities meet basic human needs
  • Identify systems thinking in traditional and modern societies
  • Evaluate what makes societies successful and sustainable

Ngā Mahi - Lesson Activities (75 minutes)

1. Ngā Pou - Society Station Exploration (25 mins)

Setup: Students rotate through 4 stations, spending 6 minutes at each, discovering the essential building blocks that all societies need to function and thrive.

Station 1: People & Communities - He Tangata

Investigation Focus:
  • Who makes up a society? How do people form communities?
  • What roles do different people play in keeping society functioning?
  • How do traditional Māori communities organize themselves?
Activities:
  • Map the different roles in your local community
  • Compare iwi structures with modern city organization
  • Identify how communities care for vulnerable members

Station 2: Governance & Decision-Making - Whakatōhea

Investigation Focus:
  • How do societies make decisions that affect everyone?
  • What different forms of leadership and governance exist?
  • How does consensus decision-making work in traditional communities?
Activities:
  • Examine hui (community meetings) vs parliament
  • Compare different leadership styles and their effects
  • Analyze how decisions get made in your school community

Station 3: Resources & Economics - Taonga & Rawa

Investigation Focus:
  • How do societies organize resources to meet everyone's needs?
  • What different economic systems exist beyond money?
  • How did traditional communities ensure no one went without?
Activities:
  • Compare gift economies with market economies
  • Investigate traditional resource sharing systems
  • Analyze how your community distributes resources

Station 4: Culture & Values - Tikanga & Uara

Investigation Focus:
  • What beliefs and values hold societies together?
  • How do traditions and customs shape daily life?
  • What happens when different cultures meet and mix?
Activities:
  • Map the cultural values of different communities
  • Examine how tikanga shapes Māori society
  • Identify cultural symbols and their meanings in various societies

2. Whakataurite - Society Systems Comparison (20 mins)

Investigation: Students analyze how different societies organize themselves by comparing traditional iwi systems with modern nation-states.

Traditional Iwi System

WHAKAPAPA (Relationships)
MANA WHENUA (Land Connection)
COLLECTIVE DECISION-MAKING
KAITIAKITANGA (Guardianship)

Relationship-based, sustainable, collective

Modern Nation-State

CITIZENSHIP (Legal Status)
TERRITORIAL BOUNDARIES
REPRESENTATIVE DEMOCRACY
RESOURCE MANAGEMENT

Rule-based, hierarchical, individual

Both Are Valid Ways of Organizing Society!

Different approaches, same goal: Creating communities where people can thrive together

3. Ngā Hiahia - What Every Society Needs (25 mins)

Systems Investigation: Students identify the universal needs that all successful societies must address, regardless of their structure.

Physical Survival

Food, water, shelter, healthcare - how does society ensure everyone's basic needs are met?

Safety & Security

Protection from harm, conflict resolution, maintaining peace - how do communities stay safe?

Education & Growth

Passing on knowledge, skills, values - how do societies prepare the next generation?

Meaning & Purpose

Spiritual life, cultural expression, belonging - how do people find meaning together?

Student Investigation Task:

Choose one societal need and research: How do three different societies (including one traditional and one modern) address this need? What can we learn from different approaches?

4. Whakaaro Rōpū - Systems Thinking About Society (5 mins)

Critical Reflection: Students apply systems thinking to understand how societies function as interconnected webs rather than isolated parts.

Systems Thinking Questions:

  • How are the four building blocks of society (people, governance, resources, culture) connected?
  • What happens when one part of a society breaks down or changes dramatically?
  • How might traditional wisdom inform improvements to modern society?
  • What societal challenges require thinking about the whole system, not just individual parts?

  • Critical Reflection: Systems thinking applied to societal challenges

Whakamutunga - Lesson Reflection

Today we discovered that societies are complex systems built on relationships, shared values, and collective decision-making. Whether we examine traditional iwi structures or modern nation-states, all successful societies must address the same fundamental human needs while respecting the dignity and contribution of every member. As we continue this unit, we'll explore how different systems of power and governance can either support or hinder human flourishing.
