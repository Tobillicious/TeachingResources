Lesson 2-1: Democracy vs Other Systems

Understanding Power, Representation & Indigenous Governance Models

Students explore different governance systems through a decolonized lens, examining how power operates in democratic, authoritarian, and indigenous systems, while questioning whose voices are heard and whose are marginalized in different forms of government.

Tino Rangatiratanga - Self-Determination & True Democracy

The concept of democracy didn't begin with ancient Greece - indigenous peoples worldwide, including Māori, had sophisticated systems of collective decision-making, consensus-building, and shared leadership for centuries. True democracy isn't just about voting every few years; it's about ensuring all voices are heard, all people are represented, and power serves the wellbeing of the community. As we examine different government systems, we ask: Who holds real power? Whose voices are amplified? Whose are silenced?

Ngā Whāinga Ako - Learning Intentions

Students Will Understand

  • Different forms of government and how they distribute power
  • Indigenous governance models as legitimate political systems
  • How government systems include or exclude different groups
  • The difference between formal and real democracy

Students Will Apply

  • Analyze power structures in different government systems
  • Compare indigenous and Western governance approaches
  • Evaluate how different systems serve community needs
  • Identify ways to make democratic systems more inclusive

Ngā Mahi - Lesson Activities (75 minutes)

1. Rangatiratanga Stations - Governance System Analysis (25 mins)

Setup: Students rotate through 4 governance stations, spending 6 minutes at each, examining how different systems organize power, make decisions, and serve (or fail to serve) their people.

Station 1: Democratic Systems - "Rule by the People"

Investigation Focus:
  • Who really gets to participate in "democratic" systems?
  • How do elections work and who gets represented?
  • What are the strengths and weaknesses of majority rule?
Activities:
  • Analyze voting patterns and representation in NZ Parliament
  • Examine historical exclusions from democracy (women, Māori, etc.)
  • Compare direct vs representative democracy examples

Station 2: Indigenous Governance - Consensus & Collective Wisdom

Investigation Focus:
  • How do traditional Māori governance systems work?
  • What role do elders, consensus, and whakapapa play?
  • How do indigenous systems balance individual and collective needs?
Activities:
  • Study iwi governance structures and hui processes
  • Compare Māori governance with other indigenous systems globally
  • Investigate contemporary co-governance arrangements in NZ

Station 3: Authoritarian Systems - Concentrated Power

Investigation Focus:
  • How do authoritarian governments maintain power?
  • What methods do they use to control information and dissent?
  • Why might some people support authoritarian leaders?
Activities:
  • Analyze colonial governments as authoritarian systems
  • Examine propaganda techniques and media control
  • Study resistance movements against authoritarian rule

Station 4: Power Analysis - Who Really Holds Control?

Investigation Focus:
  • How does economic power influence political systems?
  • What role do media, corporations, and lobbying play?
  • How can ordinary people gain and exercise political power?
Activities:
  • Map the influence of wealthy donors on political parties
  • Investigate community organizing and grassroots movements
  • Analyze how colonization concentrated power in settler hands

2. Whakataurite Kāwanatanga - Government Systems Comparison (20 mins)

Investigation: Students complete a comparative analysis matrix examining how different governance systems handle key political questions.

Key Questions Western Democracy Indigenous Governance Authoritarian Rule
Who makes decisions? Elected representatives Community consensus Single leader/small elite
How is power transferred? Regular elections Traditional processes Force/inheritance
Who gets represented? Majority (in theory) All community members Ruling group only
How are conflicts resolved? Courts/legal system Restorative justice Force/punishment

Critical Question for Students:

Which system would best serve your community's needs? What elements from different systems could be combined?

3. Whakatōmuri - When Democracy Fails: Historical Exclusions (25 mins)

Critical Investigation: Students examine how democratic systems have historically excluded marginalized groups and continue to limit true representation.

Colonial "Democracy"

Early NZ democracy excluded Māori, women, and working-class people from voting while claiming to represent "the people."

Investigation:
  • Who could vote in 1850s NZ?
  • How did property requirements exclude people?
  • What was the impact of these exclusions?

Voter Suppression Today

Modern barriers like ID laws, limited polling access, and gerrymandering can exclude marginalized communities from political power.

Investigation:
  • What makes it harder for some people to vote?
  • How do electoral systems affect representation?
  • Who benefits from limited voter participation?

Economic Democracy

Wealthy individuals and corporations can influence politics through donations, lobbying, and media ownership in ways ordinary people cannot.

Investigation:
  • How does money influence political decisions?
  • What is the role of corporate lobbying?
  • How can ordinary people compete with wealthy interests?

Indigenous Voices

Democratic systems imposed by colonizers often override indigenous governance and decision-making processes, creating ongoing conflicts.

Investigation:
  • How does Westminster democracy conflict with tino rangatiratanga?
  • What are the benefits of co-governance models?
  • How can indigenous rights be protected in democratic systems?

4. Waihanga Hou - Designing More Inclusive Democracy (5 mins)

Future-Focused Reflection: Students envision how democratic systems could be improved to better serve all people and communities.

Design Challenge Questions:

  • How could we combine the best aspects of democratic and indigenous governance?
  • What would a political system look like that truly represented all community voices?
  • How could young people have more meaningful political participation?
  • What changes would make government more responsive to community needs?

Student Challenge:

Design one specific improvement to NZ's democratic system that would make it more inclusive and effective. Be prepared to explain your reasoning.

Aromatawai - Assessment

Station Analysis Portfolio

  • Government Systems Understanding: Clear explanations of different governance models
  • Comparative Analysis: Completed comparison matrix with thoughtful insights
  • Critical Examination: Analysis of democratic exclusions and failures
  • Design Proposal: Creative solution for improving democratic participation

Extension Projects

  • Government Interview: Speak with local council members or MPs
  • Historical Research: Deep dive into voting rights movements in NZ
  • Comparative Study: Research indigenous governance in other countries
  • Action Project: Organize a school-wide democratic decision-making process

Whakamutunga - Lesson Reflection

Today we discovered that no single governance system has all the answers, and that truly democratic societies require constant vigilance to ensure all voices are heard and represented. Western democratic systems, while valuable, are not the only legitimate forms of government - indigenous governance models offer important insights about consensus-building, collective responsibility, and sustainable decision-making. As we continue examining systems of power, we must ask not just "Is this system democratic?" but "Does this system serve the wellbeing of all people and the environment?"
