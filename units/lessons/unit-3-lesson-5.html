Unit 3, Lesson 5: Traditional Mathematics & Modern Applications

Māori Mathematical Concepts Meet Contemporary Problem Solving & Technology

Students explore how traditional Māori mathematical thinking and problem-solving approaches connect to and enhance modern mathematical applications, demonstrating the sophistication and continued relevance of indigenous mathematical knowledge.

Lesson Overview

Duration

60 minutes

Year Level

Years 8-10

Curriculum Areas

Mathematics, Technology, Science, Visual Arts

Karakia & Cultural Opening (5 minutes)

Opening Karakia for Mathematical Wisdom

"Kia mārama ai te taiao
I nga tapatoru, i nga raina
I nga mea katoa e kitea ai"

So that the natural world may be clear
In its triangles, in its lines
In all things that can be seen

Mathematical Thinking as Universal Language

Today we explore how mathematical thinking transcends cultural boundaries while being expressed uniquely within different cultural contexts. We will see how traditional Māori mathematical concepts continue to offer valuable approaches to modern problem-solving.

Ngā Whāinga Ako - Learning Objectives

Knowledge & Understanding

  • Recognize traditional Māori mathematical concepts and their sophistication
  • Understand how mathematical thinking appears across all cultures
  • Identify mathematical patterns in traditional art, architecture, and practices
  • Connect traditional mathematical thinking to modern applications

Skills & Application

  • Apply traditional mathematical concepts to solve modern problems
  • Analyze geometric patterns and proportional relationships
  • Use different counting systems and mathematical representations
  • Integrate traditional and modern mathematical approaches

Traditional Māori Mathematical Concepts (20 minutes)

Mathematics as Cultural Expression & Practical Tool

Traditional Māori mathematical thinking emerges from practical needs - navigation, construction, resource management, and artistic expression - but demonstrates sophisticated understanding of mathematical principles that parallel and sometimes exceed Western mathematical concepts.

Counting Systems & Number Concepts

  • Base-10 System: Sophisticated counting using body parts (toes, fingers)
  • Quantity Expressions: Precise terms for specific amounts and groupings
  • Proportional Thinking: Understanding ratios in traditional practices
  • Estimation Methods: Practical techniques for quick quantity assessment
  • Calendar Mathematics: Complex lunar and seasonal calculations

Geometric Principles & Spatial Reasoning

  • Sacred Geometry: Mathematical relationships in spiritual and practical architecture
  • Pattern Recognition: Complex geometric sequences in traditional art
  • Symmetry Understanding: Bilateral and radial symmetry in design
  • Proportion Systems: Mathematical relationships in human and natural forms
  • Spatial Navigation: Three-dimensional thinking for oceanic travel

Mathematical Concepts in Traditional Practices

Fibonacci in Koru

The traditional koru spiral follows mathematical principles identical to the Fibonacci sequence found in many natural growth patterns.

Golden Ratio in Wharenui

Traditional meeting house proportions often reflect the golden ratio, creating aesthetically pleasing and structurally sound buildings.

Trigonometry in Navigation

Traditional ocean navigation uses angle calculations and trigonometric principles to determine direction and distance using stars.

Fractals in Tukutuku

Traditional weaving patterns demonstrate fractal geometry, with self-similar patterns repeated at different scales.

Modern Mathematical Applications & Connections (15 minutes)

Traditional Concepts in Contemporary Technology

Many mathematical concepts embedded in traditional Māori culture have direct applications in modern technology, engineering, and scientific research, demonstrating the timeless nature of mathematical principles.

Traditional Mathematical Thinking

  • Pattern recognition in natural systems
  • Proportional relationships in design
  • Geometric sequences in art and architecture
  • Spatial reasoning for navigation
  • Estimation and approximation methods

Modern Technology Applications

  • Computer pattern recognition algorithms
  • Engineering design optimization
  • Fractal geometry in computer graphics
  • GPS and satellite navigation systems
  • Machine learning and data approximation

Traditional → Modern Application Examples

Koru Spirals → Computer Graphics

The mathematical principles of koru spirals are used in computer graphics algorithms for creating natural-looking growth patterns and animations.

Navigation Methods → GPS Technology

Traditional celestial navigation principles inform modern GPS algorithms and provide backup navigation methods for technology failures.

Tukutuku Patterns → Architectural Design

Traditional weaving patterns inspire modern architectural facades and structural engineering solutions using fractal geometry.

Proportional Systems → Design Software

Traditional proportional relationships are embedded in modern design software to create aesthetically pleasing and functional layouts.

Hands-On Investigation: Mathematical Pattern Analysis (20 minutes)

Traditional Pattern → Modern Application Project

Working in pairs, choose one traditional Māori mathematical concept and explore its connections to modern applications:

Investigation Options

  • Koru Spirals: Fibonacci sequences and growth patterns
  • Tukutuku Geometry: Fractal patterns and tessellations
  • Wharenui Proportions: Golden ratio and architectural design
  • Star Navigation: Trigonometry and coordinate systems
  • Traditional Counting: Number bases and computational systems

Investigation Process

  1. Analyze: Identify mathematical principles in traditional concept
  2. Research: Find modern applications of these principles
  3. Calculate: Use math to demonstrate the connections
  4. Create: Design something new using both approaches
  5. Present: Share your findings and creation

Tools & Resources

  • Digital Tools: Graphing calculators, geometry software, coding apps
  • Traditional Tools: Compass, ruler, protractor, grid paper
  • Creative Materials: Art supplies for creating patterns
  • Research Access: Library databases, mathematical websites

Integration Questions

  • What mathematical principles are embedded in your chosen traditional concept?
  • How are these same principles used in modern technology or applications?
  • What can modern mathematics learn from traditional approaches?
  • How could you combine traditional and modern mathematical thinking to solve new problems?

Problem Solving Integration: Traditional + Modern Approaches (15 minutes)

Challenge: Designing a Community Garden

Your school wants to create a traditional Māori garden that also incorporates modern sustainability technology. Use both traditional mathematical principles and modern calculation methods to design the optimal layout.

Traditional Design Principles

  • Sacred geometry in garden layout
  • Proportional relationships between plants
  • Seasonal timing for planting and harvesting
  • Traditional companion planting patterns
  • Water flow and drainage using natural contours

Modern Mathematical Tools

  • Calculate optimal spacing using area formulas
  • Design irrigation systems using flow rates
  • Model sun exposure using trigonometry
  • Optimize plant yields using data analysis
  • Plan maintenance schedules using probability

Design Requirements

  • Space: 20m × 15m available area
  • Plants: Include 5 traditional Māori crops
  • Technology: Solar-powered irrigation system
  • Accessibility: Wheelchair-accessible pathways
  • Sustainability: Minimize water usage and maximize yield

Integration Process

  1. Use traditional principles to choose overall layout and proportions
  2. Apply modern mathematics to calculate specific measurements
  3. Combine both approaches to optimize the design
  4. Present your integrated solution with mathematical justification

Whakaata - Reflection & Assessment (10 minutes)

Exit Ticket: Mathematical Integration Understanding

Complete the following reflection questions:

  1. Mathematical Sophistication: Give three examples of sophisticated mathematical thinking in traditional Māori culture.
  2. Modern Connections: Explain how one traditional mathematical concept connects to modern technology or applications.
  3. Problem-Solving Approaches: How might combining traditional and modern mathematical thinking create better solutions to problems?
  4. Cultural Mathematics: What does exploring traditional mathematics teach us about the universality of mathematical thinking?
  5. Personal Application: How could you apply traditional mathematical principles in your own projects or interests?

Assessment Criteria

  • Recognition: Identifies mathematical sophistication in traditional concepts
  • Connection: Links traditional mathematical thinking to modern applications
  • Integration: Combines different mathematical approaches effectively
  • Application: Uses mathematical concepts to solve practical problems

Whakakapi - Closing Reflection

"He tapatoru, he whare kōrero" - A triangle is a house of knowledge. Today we have seen that mathematical thinking is truly universal, appearing in all cultures but expressed uniquely within each tradition. Traditional Māori mathematical concepts are not primitive precursors to "real" mathematics - they are sophisticated approaches that continue to offer valuable insights for modern problem-solving. When we combine traditional mathematical wisdom with contemporary tools, we create more powerful and culturally grounded solutions.

Kia kaha te whakaro - strong thinking!
