Unit 4, Lesson 1: Indigenous Scientific Methods

Observation, Pattern Recognition, and a Science of Place

Lesson Overview

This lesson introduces students to the concept of indigenous science, demonstrating that careful observation, pattern recognition, and intergenerational knowledge transfer are a valid and powerful scientific methodology. We will explore how Māori and other indigenous peoples have used their scientific methods to thrive for centuries.

Learning Activities

1. Do Now: What is Science? (10 mins)

In pairs, students brainstorm a definition of "science". What does it involve? Who does it? What is it for? Share ideas as a class.

2. Introduction to Indigenous Science (15 mins)

Class discussion: Introduce the idea that science is not just one thing. It's a way of understanding the world, and different cultures have developed different, equally valid, scientific methods. Introduce the concepts of place-based knowledge and oral tradition.

3. Case Study: Navigating by the Stars (20 mins)

Hand out the Māori Astronomy & Navigation handout. Students read through it and answer the critical thinking questions. Discuss how the star compass is a powerful scientific model.

4. Exit Ticket (5 mins)

Students write down one way that Māori astronomy is similar to modern science and one way it is different.

Lesson Details

Learning Intentions:

  • Understand that indigenous knowledge is a form of science.
  • Identify key principles of indigenous scientific methods.
  • Analyse a specific example of Māori science.

Key Competencies:

  • Thinking
  • Using language, symbols, and texts
