Lesson 2: Te PEEL Method - Building Powerful Arguments

Using structured paragraphs to create compelling, culturally grounded arguments

He Taonga - Cultural Foundation

In Te Ao Māori, powerful speaking (kōrero) and persuasion are honored skills. From marae debates to contemporary activism, Māori have always understood that strong arguments require structure, evidence, and connection to values. Today we learn the PEEL method as one way to organize our thoughts for maximum impact.

Key Connections: Traditional debate (whaikōrero), contemporary Māori advocacy, Treaty negotiations, community leadership

Ngā Whāinga Ako - Learning Intentions

  • Structure: Understand and apply the PEEL paragraph method (Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link)
  • Analysis: Deconstruct arguments to identify their structural components
  • Cultural Application: Use PEEL to argue for issues important to our communities and Aotearoa
  • Transfer Skills: Apply argument structure across subjects (history, science, social issues)

Curriculum Connections

English (Level 5)

  • Speaking, Writing, and Presenting: Organize ideas for specific purposes
  • Use vocabulary and structures to create meaning and effect

Cross-Curricular

  • Social Studies: Analyze historical arguments and evidence
  • Science: Structure scientific explanations and hypotheses
  • Te Reo Māori: Traditional persuasion techniques

Ngā Mahi - Lesson Activities (75 minutes)

1. Tīmata - Cultural Issue Stance (10 mins)

Enhanced Cultural Context: Present ākonga with contemporary issues affecting Māori communities and Aotearoa. Examples:

  • "Te Reo Māori should be compulsory in all NZ schools"
  • "Climate change action should prioritize Māori environmental knowledge"
  • "All councils should have dedicated Māori ward representation"

Students write one sentence supporting and one opposing, each with cultural reasoning. Share perspectives as a whānau (class).

Differentiation:
  • Support: Provide sentence starters: "I believe... because in Te Ao Māori..."
  • Extension: Include specific examples from current events or local community

2. Ako Atahua - PEEL Structure with Cultural Examples (20 mins)

Cultural Integration: Introduce PEEL as a method used in:

  • Treaty negotiations and Waitangi Tribunal claims
  • Contemporary Māori political speeches and advocacy
  • Marae debates and community discussions

Activity Steps:

  1. Distribute enhanced PEEL Method handout with cultural examples
  2. Analyze a PEEL paragraph about a Te Tiriti issue together
  3. Color-code components: Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link
  4. Discuss how this structure supports Māori advocacy and tino rangatiratanga
Cross-Curricular Connections:
  • History: How historical arguments were structured in Treaty debates
  • Science: How scientists use similar evidence-based reasoning
  • Te Reo: Traditional oratory techniques (whakataukī, whakapapa)

3. Mahi-ā-Rōpū - Community Issue Advocacy (40 mins)

Authentic Context: Groups choose real community issues affecting their whānau, hapū, or local area. Examples:

  • Environmental protection in local waterways
  • Youth mental health support in communities
  • Housing affordability for whānau
  • Cultural education opportunities in schools

Enhanced Tasks:

  1. Kaupapa Selection (15 mins): Groups research and select an authentic community issue, formulating their main argument (tā rātou kōrero matua)
  2. PEEL Paragraph Creation (25 mins): Each member writes one PEEL paragraph supporting their kaupapa, incorporating:
    • Cultural values and perspectives
    • Real evidence from credible sources
    • Community impact analysis
    • Call to action linking to next steps
Differentiation Strategies:
  • Visual Learners: Create infographic versions of PEEL paragraphs
  • Kinesthetic: Act out debate scenarios using PEEL structure
  • Advanced: Include counter-arguments and rebuttals (PEEL-R)
  • Support: Provide cultural example bank and sentence starters

4. Whakatau - Community Voice Sharing (5 mins)

Authentic Assessment: Each group presents their "Point" as if addressing a community hui or council meeting. Audience (class whānau) provides constructive feedback using these criteria:

  • Clarity: Can we clearly understand your main argument?
  • Cultural Relevance: Does this connect to our values and community needs?
  • Impact Potential: Could this argument create positive change?
Extension Opportunity: Groups can volunteer to present their full arguments to actual community groups, school board, or local council - connecting classroom learning to real advocacy.

Aromatawai - Assessment & Next Steps

Formative Assessment

  • PEEL component identification accuracy
  • Cultural integration in arguments
  • Peer feedback quality and cultural respect
  • Engagement with community issues

Transfer Opportunities

  • History: Analyze Treaty negotiation arguments
  • Science: Structure environmental impact explanations
  • Social Studies: Evaluate political campaign messages
  • Te Reo: Traditional debate and oratory forms

Whakaaro - Cultural Reflection

Today we learned that strong arguments require structure, just as traditional Māori speeches follow protocols and patterns that make them powerful. The PEEL method gives us one framework for organizing our thoughts when we advocate for our communities and values.

Key Insight: Whether speaking on a marae, in Parliament, or in our classrooms, the goal is the same - to communicate our truth in ways that create understanding and positive change for our people.
