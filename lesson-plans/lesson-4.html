Lesson 4: Deconstructing Visual Texts

Analysing Political Cartoons and other Visual Media.

Learning Intentions (Ngā Whāinga Ako)

  • To identify key techniques used in visual texts (e.g., Symbolism, Caricature, Colour, Framing).
  • To analyse how these techniques are used to convey a persuasive message.
  • To begin planning the visual component of the campaign project.

NZC Links (Level 5)

  • **Listening, Reading, and Viewing:** "Show an increasing understanding of how the combination of verbal and visual features communicates ideas and information."
  • **Speaking, Writing, and Presenting:** "Combine verbal and visual features to create meaning and effect."

Cultural Competencies (Tātaiako)

  • Wānanga: Participating in shared analysis and deconstruction of visual texts.
  • Ako: Valuing students' own interpretations and cultural understanding of symbols and images.

Lesson Activities (75 Minutes)

1. Whakatau / Do Now: Reading the Image (5 mins)

Show students a simple, common symbol (e.g., a pōhutukawa flower at Christmas, a recycling logo). In their books, they write down what the symbol means to them and what ideas or feelings it evokes. Discuss the cultural context of symbols.

2. Explicit Teaching: The Language of Visuals (20 mins)

Teacher introduces visual media as a form of persuasive text that has its own language.

  • Distribute the Political Cartoon Analysis handout.
  • Discuss the four key techniques (Symbolism, Caricature, Labelling, Irony), using the examples on the sheet. Broaden the discussion to include colour, framing, and composition from the Elements of Art Handout.
  • As a class, work through the deconstruction and analysis tasks (Questions 1 & 2) on the cartoon handout.

3. Project Work: Planning the Visual Campaign (45 mins)

Students get into their campaign project groups.

  • Task 1 (20 mins): Groups decide on the format for their final visual text (e.g., poster, cartoon, short video). They will create a storyboard or a rough sketch of their visual.
  • Task 2 (25 mins): On their sketch/storyboard, groups must label the specific visual techniques they plan to use to convey their message (e.g., "Symbol: a broken chain to represent freedom," "Colour: red used to show anger or urgency").

Differentiation:

  • Support: Provide a template with boxes for "Main Image," "Slogan," "Key Symbol," and "Colour Palette" to help structure their planning.
  • Extension: Challenge students to create two different visual concepts for the same message and be prepared to explain which one is more effective and why.

4. Whakakapi / Gallery Walk (5 mins)

One person from each group does a "gallery walk" to look at the other groups' visual plans. They return to their group and share one interesting idea or technique they saw that could inspire their own work.
