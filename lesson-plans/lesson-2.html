Lesson 2: Building a Strong Argument

Using the PEEL method to structure persuasive paragraphs.

Learning Intentions (Ngā Whāinga Ako)

  • To understand the components of the PEEL paragraph structure (Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link).
  • To deconstruct a paragraph to identify its PEEL components.
  • To apply the PEEL method to write a structured, persuasive paragraph.

NZC Links (Level 5)

  • **Speaking, Writing, and Presenting:** "Organise and structure ideas and information for a particular purpose or effect."
  • **Speaking, Writing, and Presenting:** "Use a range of vocabulary and sentence structures to create meaning and effect."

Cultural Competencies (Tātaiako)

  • Manaakitanga: Respecting and acknowledging the diverse viewpoints shared during the 'Agree or Disagree' activity.
  • Ako: Co-constructing understanding of a strong argument by deconstructing an example together as a class.

Resources

Writer's Toolkit: The PEEL Method

Lesson Activities (75 Minutes)

1. Whakatau / Do Now: Agree or Disagree? (10 mins)

Present students with a provocative statement (e.g., "School holidays should be shorter," "All students should learn a musical instrument"). In their books, they write one sentence agreeing and one sentence disagreeing, each with a reason. Validate all contributions and facilitate a brief class discussion on why people hold different views.

2. Explicit Teaching: Introducing PEEL - The Tūāpapa of Argument (20 mins)

Teacher introduces the PEEL method as a powerful tūāpapa (foundation) for structuring a logical and convincing argument.

  • Distribute the Writer's Toolkit: The PEEL Method handout.
  • Go through the "Deconstructing a PEEL Paragraph" section as a class, explaining each component clearly.
  • As a class, complete the deconstruction activity (Question 1) on the handout, colour-coding the different parts of the paragraph on the board.

3. Project Work: Choosing a Kaupapa & Crafting a Point (40 mins)

Students get into their campaign project groups.

  • Task 1 (15 mins): Groups must decide on their final campaign topic (kaupapa) from their brainstorm list. They should formulate their overall campaign message into a single, clear sentence (their main contention).
  • Task 2 (25 mins): Each group member chooses one argument that supports their main contention and uses the PEEL framework to write a single, powerful paragraph. They can use the application task (Question 2) on the handout for practice and structure. Encourage them to invent reasonable evidence for now.

Differentiation:

  • Support: Provide sentence starters for each part of the PEEL structure (e.g., "The main point I want to make is...", "For example...", "This shows that...", "Therefore...").
  • Extension: Challenge students to write a second PEEL paragraph that argues a counter-point to their first one. This builds critical thinking and the ability to anticipate opposing views.

4. Whakakapi / Exit Ticket (5 mins)

Each group shares the "Point" sentence from one of their paragraphs. The teacher provides feedback on its clarity and strength as a topic sentence.
