Lesson 3: The Art of Persuasion

Using Rhetorical Devices: Ethos, Pathos, and Logos.

Learning Intentions

  • To define and identify the three artistic proofs: Ethos, Pathos, and Logos.
  • To analyse how a speaker uses these devices to create a persuasive effect.
  • To apply these devices to strengthen their own persuasive writing.

NZC Links (Level 5)

  • **Listening, Reading, and Viewing:** "Analyse how language features are used to create meaning and effect in a range of texts."
  • **Speaking, Writing, and Presenting:** "Select, develop, and communicate ideas on a range of topics, making deliberate language choices to create an impact."

Resources

Writer's Toolkit: Rhetorical Devices Speech Analysis Handout

Lesson Activities (75 Minutes)

1. Do Now: Spot the Technique (10 mins)

Show students three short, powerful advertisements (e.g., from SPCA, a car company, a technology brand). For each, students must identify the primary emotion or idea the ad is trying to evoke (e.g., sympathy, trust, logic).

2. Explicit Teaching: Introducing Ethos, Pathos, Logos (20 mins)

Teacher introduces the three artistic proofs as the building blocks of persuasion.

  • Distribute the Rhetorical Devices handout. Explain each concept clearly, using the examples on the sheet.
  • As a class, complete the deconstruction activity (Question 1) on the handout.
  • Briefly introduce the Speech Analysis Handout and explain that they will be using these skills to deconstruct a full speech.

3. Project Work: Weaving in Persuasion (40 mins)

Students get into their campaign project groups with their PEEL paragraphs from the previous lesson.

  • Task 1 (20 mins): Groups analyse the "Empty Playground" speech excerpt on the Speech Analysis handout, identifying examples of Ethos, Pathos, and Logos.
  • Task 2 (20 mins): Groups take their own PEEL paragraphs and work to enhance them by deliberately weaving in at least one example of Ethos, Pathos, or Logos. They should aim to make their arguments more compelling.

4. Plenary / Exit Ticket (5 mins)

Each group shares one "before" and "after" sentence, explaining how their addition of a rhetorical device made their argument stronger.

Differentiation

Scaffolding (Support)

  • Provide a "bank" of emotional words (for Pathos), expert types (for Ethos, e.g., "scientists," "researchers"), and logical phrases (for Logos, e.g., "it is logical that," "the data shows").
  • Use a colour-coding system: ask students to highlight examples of Ethos in one colour, Pathos in another, and Logos in a third on their handouts.

Extension (Advanced)

  • Challenge students to analyze a short, real-world speech (e.g., a TED Talk, a political speech) and identify how the speaker balances the three appeals.
  • Ask them to rewrite one of their PEEL paragraphs from three different perspectives, with each version focusing on a different appeal (one heavy on Pathos, one on Ethos, one on Logos).
