Lesson 11: Unit Reflection & Next Steps

Consolidating learning and looking forward.

Learning Intentions (Ngā Whāinga Ako)

  • To accurately self-assess one's own work against success criteria.
  • To reflect on the process of giving and receiving peer feedback.
  • To identify key skills learned during the unit and consider their future application.

NZC Links (Level 5)

  • **Thinking:** "Access and select information, and think critically about it." (Applied to self-assessment).
  • **Participating and Contributing:** "Explore how they and others can participate and contribute to communities."

Cultural Competencies (Tātaiako)

  • Ako: Taking full responsibility for their learning journey by reflecting on their process, progress, and outcomes.
  • Manaakitanga: Reviewing peer feedback with an open and appreciative mindset.

Resources

Assessment Rubric

Student campaigns and written peer feedback from Lesson 10.

Lesson Activities (75 Minutes)

1. Whakatau / Do Now: My Biggest Takeaway (10 mins)

Students individually complete the reflection from the previous lesson: "The most important thing I have learned about using my voice to create change is..." In their project groups, they share their answers and discuss common themes.

2. Guided Practice: Self-Assessment (25 mins)

Teacher guides students through the process of self-assessment, emphasizing that it is a crucial skill for lifelong learning.

  • Hand back the written peer feedback from the previous lesson.
  • Using the Assessment Rubric, students individually highlight where they believe their work sits for each criterion.
  • In the margins, they must write a short justification for their self-assessment, referencing specific parts of their campaign and the peer feedback they received.

3. Independent Reflection: What Now? (35 mins)

Students complete a final individual reflection in their books. This is a quiet, focused activity.

  • What? What was the most challenging part of this project for you personally?
  • So What? What skills did you develop to overcome this challenge? What did you learn about yourself as a learner?
  • Now What? How could you use the skills from this "Persuasive Campaign" project in other subjects, or in your life outside of school? Be specific.

Differentiation:

  • Support: Provide sentence starters for the reflection questions (e.g., "The most challenging part was...", "To overcome this, I learned to...", "I could use my speech writing skills in...").
  • Extension: Challenge students to write a short letter to a real-world organisation or person of influence about their campaign issue, using their new persuasive skills.

4. Whakakapi / Closing the Loop (5 mins)

Teacher leads a final brief discussion on the importance of social action and reminds students that the skills they have practiced are the first step to making a real difference in their communities. Collect the self-assessed rubrics.
