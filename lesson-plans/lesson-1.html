Lesson 1: Introduction to Persuasion

Unpacking the unit project and the fundamentals of persuasive language.

Learning Intentions (Ngā Whāinga Ako)

  • To understand the goals and assessment requirements of the "Persuasive Campaign" unit project.
  • To define the three main purposes of writing (PIE: Persuade, Inform, Entertain).
  • To identify the author's purpose in a range of short texts.

NZC Links (Level 5)

  • **Listening, Reading, and Viewing:** "Show an increasing understanding of how texts are shaped for different purposes and audiences."
  • **Speaking, Writing, and Presenting:** "Select, develop, and communicate ideas on a range of topics."

Cultural Competencies (Tātaiako)

  • Whanaungatanga: Building collaborative relationships by establishing project groups and sharing ideas.
  • Ako: Recognising that all students bring valuable prior knowledge about persuasion to the classroom.

Lesson Activities (75 Minutes)

1. Whakatau / Do Now: What is Persuasion? (10 mins)

Begin with the whakataukī: "Ehara taku toa i te toa takitahi, engari he toa takitini." (My strength is not as an individual, but as a collective). Explain this relates to how we will work together in this unit.

In pairs, students brainstorm a definition for "persuasion" and list examples of persuasive texts (e.g., ads, movie trailers, opinion articles). As a class, co-construct a shared definition on the board.

2. Explicit Teaching: Unpacking the Kaupapa (15 mins)

Teacher introduces the "Critical Literacy & Persuasion" unit and the final "Persuasive Campaign" project.

  • Distribute and read through the Unit Project Brief as a class.
  • Clarify the three components: Speech, Visual Text, and Rationale.
  • Introduce the PIE framework (Persuade, Inform, Entertain) using the Author's Purpose Handout. Explain that while texts have a primary purpose, they often contain elements of others.

3. Guided Practice & Project Work: Campaign Brainstorm (45 mins)

Students are placed into their project groups (3-4 students).

  • Task 1 (20 mins): In groups, students complete the "PIE Practice" section of the Author's Purpose handout.
  • Task 2 (25 mins): Groups begin brainstorming potential topics (kaupapa) for their campaign. They should aim for a list of 3-5 social or environmental issues they are passionate about. Encourage them to think about issues relevant to their school, community (whānau), or country.

Differentiation:

  • Support: Provide a list of potential topics for groups who are struggling to brainstorm. Work with these groups to select a topic.
  • Extension: Challenge early-finishing groups to consider the ideal audience for each of their brainstormed topics. Who are they trying to persuade?

4. Whakakapi / Exit Ticket (5 mins)

Each student writes on a sticky note one whakaaro (idea/issue) they are most excited to work on for their campaign and sticks it on the board on their way out. This gives the teacher a quick overview of popular topics.
