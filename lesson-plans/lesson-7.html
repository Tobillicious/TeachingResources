Lesson 7: Visual Campaign Workshop

Creating the visual component of the persuasive campaign.

Learning Intentions (Ngā Whāinga Ako)

  • To apply knowledge of visual language techniques to create a persuasive visual text (pikitia).
  • To work collaboratively to produce a visual component that aligns with the campaign's overall message.
  • To manage time effectively to complete a key component of the project.

NZC Links (Level 5)

  • **Speaking, Writing, and Presenting:** "Combine verbal and visual features to create meaning and effect, and justify the choices made."
  • **Visual Arts:** "Develop and clarify ideas, using a range of conventions from established practice."

Cultural Competencies (Tātaiako)

  • Whanaungatanga: Collaborating effectively as a group to bring a shared creative vision to life.
  • Tangata Whenuatanga: Creating visuals that reflect students' own identities, cultures, and perspectives on the chosen issue.

Plus art supplies (paper, markers, coloured pencils) or digital tools (Canva, Adobe Express, video editing software).

Lesson Activities (75 Minutes)

1. Whakatau / Do Now: Visual Hook (5 mins)

Show students a powerful and well-known Aotearoa-specific visual (e.g., the 'Nuclear Free NZ' logo, a famous tourism poster). In one sentence, students describe the main message the visual is trying to send.

2. Explicit Teaching: Review & Goal Setting (10 mins)

Briefly review the key visual techniques (Symbolism, Colour, Framing, etc.). Remind students that the goal of their visual is to support and enhance the argument made in their speech. It should be able to communicate the core message even without the speech.

Teacher sets a clear goal for the lesson: "By the end of this lesson, every group must have a completed first draft of their visual component (pikitia)."

3. Project Work: Visual Creation Workshop (55 mins)

This is a dedicated work session. Students get into their campaign project groups and work on creating their visual text.

  • Groups work from their plans and storyboards from Lesson 4.
  • Teacher circulates, acting as a facilitator and providing feedback. Key questions to ask groups (Teaching as Inquiry):
    • "How does this visual connect to your main message (kaupapa)?"
    • "What symbol have you used and why is it meaningful for your audience?"
    • "How does your choice of colour affect the mood (wairua)?"
  • Ensure groups are managing their time effectively and that all members are contributing.

Differentiation:

  • Support: Provide a checklist for the visual text, ensuring it includes a clear focal point, a slogan, and use of at least two symbolic colours.
  • Extension: Challenge students to create a short "artist's statement" to accompany their visual, explaining their choices in more detail.

4. Whakakapi / Gallery Walk (5 mins)

Each group places their visual draft on their desk. Students do a silent "gallery walk" around the room. They are given one sticky note to leave a piece of positive and specific feedback ("I like how you used...") on another group's work.
