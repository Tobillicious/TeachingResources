Lesson 10: Campaign Presentations

Showcasing and evaluating the persuasive campaigns.

Learning Intentions

  • To deliver a polished and fluent persuasive speech as part of a group.
  • To present a cohesive campaign that integrates a speech and a visual text.
  • To critically and respectfully evaluate the work of peers.

NZC Links (Level 5)

  • **Speaking, Writing, and Presenting:** "Deliver a fluent and coherent oral text that is organised and structured for a particular purpose, and justifies the choices made."
  • **Listening, Reading, and Viewing:** "Analyse and evaluate how verbal and non-verbal language is used to create meaning and effect in a range of oral, written, and visual texts."

Resources

Unit Project Brief (for assessment criteria)

Completed student campaigns (speeches, visuals, rationales).

Lesson Activities (75 Minutes)

Note: These presentations may take place over several lessons, depending on class size. This structure is for one 75-minute block.

1. Do Now: Setting the Stage (5 mins)

Students get into their groups for a final, brief run-through of their presentation order and to set up any required technology.

2. Campaign Presentations (60 mins)

Each group delivers their persuasive campaign to the class. The structure for each presentation should be:

  • Briefly introduce their topic and campaign goal.
  • Present their visual text (show poster, play video, etc.).
  • Deliver their 2-3 minute persuasive speech.

Audience Task: While other groups are presenting, students are to act as a critical audience. For each campaign, they should write down:

  • What was the single most persuasive part of the campaign?
  • One question they have for the group.

3. Q&A and Peer Feedback (5 mins per group)

After each presentation, the teacher facilitates a brief Q&A session where the audience can ask their questions. The teacher provides summative feedback based on the assessment criteria in the project brief.

4. Plenary / Unit Reflection (5 mins)

As a final reflection on the unit, students complete the following sentence in their books: "The most important thing I have learned about persuasion is..."
